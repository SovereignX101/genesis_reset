A core requirement of Sovereign Computational Economics is the ability to process economic events deterministically at scale. To evaluate this capability, a series of stress tests were executed on consumer‑grade hardware using the **Substrate Binary** and **SPINE**. These tests simulate continuous **deposit and withdrawal events**, exercising the full constitutional mutation pipeline:

\[
\text{Event} \rightarrow \text{EIP} \rightarrow \text{EIA} \rightarrow \text{Synthyosis} \rightarrow \text{Portfolio} \rightarrow \text{Invariants} \rightarrow \text{State}
\]

Each event represents a synthetic deposit or redemption, ensuring that the entire economic metabolism of the organism is tested under load.

## **Test Conditions**

The benchmark was executed on **standard consumer hardware**, specifically:

- **Device:** Acer laptop (retail, non‑specialised hardware)  
- **CPU:** 8‑core processor (16 logical threads)  
- **Execution Threads:** 8 substrate threads pinned to physical cores  
- **Memory:** 32 GB RAM  
- **Storage:** NVMe SSD (event log persistence)  
- **Operating System:** Windows 11  
- **Build:** Release‑mode Substrate Binary with invariant checks enabled  
- **Workload:** Continuous synthetic **deposit + redemption** events  
- **Replay Cycles:** Full deterministic replays to verify state identity  

Threading was deliberately constrained to 8 physical cores to eliminate OS scheduling noise. No batching, hyper‑threading optimisation, or speculative execution was used.
